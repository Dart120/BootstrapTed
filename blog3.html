ARE 4-YEAR-OLDS BETTER AT SCIENTIFIC DISCOVERY THAN UNIVERSITY STUDENTS?

In one experiment, researchers presented kindergarteners and undergraduates with toy blocks, some of which caused a machine to flash a light and play music. The children were open to the idea that abnormal combinations of blocks could turn the machine on, whereas the university students remained fixated on the obvious solution—that the shape of individual blocks made the music play—even in the face of evidence that it was wrong. It might seem strange but this is just one of a plethora of studies that show children to be better at exploring unlikely possibilities, while adults are stubbornly inclined to build on existing knowledge. In a way, children are more skillful scientists, open to novel hypotheses.
This is the premise of Alison Gopnik’s TED talk “What do babies think?” She argues that children are more intelligent, more subtle, and more accurate in learning than we ever would’ve thought. To illuminate the ways in which our brains lose plasticity, Gopnik contrasts how adult consciousness differs from that of a baby’s. Our attention functions like a spotlight: what we need to focus on is vivid and everything we deem irrelevant falls away. Our attention is entirely purpose driven. Babies, however, have more of a “lantern of consciousness” than a spotlight. They’re considerably worse at narrowing down their attention to one thing but they far exceed us in taking in a myriad of information from multiple sources.
Gopnik’s expertise in both psychology and philosophy makes for a surprisingly touching exploration of perennial questions like how we become the adults that we are. Although the focus is on children the talk delves into what it means to have creativity and imagination. If you’re interested in how our minds work in our earliest, formative days, and what we lose as we get older, this talk and the rest of Gopnik’s work is worth your time. You’ll love the way she tackled these questions—her willingness to cut across conventional academic subjects, her blending of qualitative and quantitative methods and her charismatic presence makes for a hugely entertaining watch.

Global Policy at 10

I recently received an invitation from Blanche de Biolley, Co-President of Durham University’s TEDx, to write about Global Policy Journal based at DU’s School of Government and International Affairs that includes key figures from Harvard, KCL and LSE. The Journal completed its tenth year in 2019 and has entered a new decade in a new post-Brexit world.
The Journal focuses on the notion of an interconnected world that generates a wealth of experiences. As the Journal’s founders we attempted to systematically capture the politics and policies of a post-World War II globalised world.
We interpret ‘global’ to mean a unit of space that covers the geopolitical areas of the planet. We also deem ‘global’ as a receptacle for the idea of ‘shared communities of fate’. We sought to bring together research into transnational and multifaceted policy making. We explore old and emerging challenges, ranging from nuclear proliferation and climate change to the governance of AI and gene technology.
Once regarded as an unusual journal within a traditionally more austere world of paper publishing, today we are part of a dynamic and ever-changing landscape of knowledge production. Ten years on, we can look back on receiving a coveted Journal Impact Factor within a record four years of launching.
We are now in a world where we are increasingly accustomed on the focus on what we do not share; the vocabulary of divided countries, populism and ethnonationalism, framed in both positive and negative ways. The frictions and factions that threaten international collaboration over areas of shared concern – climate change, global security, pandemics and many more.
Our own agenda is not about the search for silver bullets that can solve these complex problems. Our agenda is about reflecting and furthering world class rigorous research. Global Policy can and will continue to play a vital role in offering and opening the space for meaningful debates and discourses on key policy issues across the world.
